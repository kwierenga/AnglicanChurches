## Summary
The ruins of St. John the Evangelist stand near Black River in St. Elizabeth parish. Once an active Anglican church, the building fell into disuse and now survives only as stone walls and fragments — a quiet monument to the area's colonial past.

## History
Black River was a prosperous town in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, enriched by the logwood and sugar trades carried along the Black River waterway. Anglican churches were established across the parish to serve planters, merchants, and the wider community.

St. John the Evangelist was one such church. As economic conditions changed and populations shifted, the church fell out of regular use. Over time, without maintenance, the building deteriorated. Today only the ruined walls remain, overgrown and exposed to the elements.

The ruins are a reminder of how Jamaica's settlement patterns have changed over the centuries, with once-thriving communities declining as trade routes and industries shifted.

## Structure/Architecture
What remains suggests a modest stone church of typical colonial-era construction — thick limestone walls with window and door openings still partially visible. The roof, interior fittings, and any wooden elements have long since disappeared. Vegetation has reclaimed much of the site.

## Worship & Clergy
No services are held at this site. It is no longer a functioning church.

## Interesting Facts
- Black River was one of the first towns in Jamaica to receive electric street lighting, reflecting its former prosperity.
- The ruins are one of several abandoned church sites in rural St. Elizabeth.
- The Black River itself is a popular destination for boat tours and crocodile spotting.
