## Summary
St. James Parish Church stands on a hill overlooking Montego Bay and is one of the most prominent historic churches on Jamaica's north coast. The current building dates from the early nineteenth century.

## History
An Anglican church has served the parish of St. James since the late seventeenth century. The present stone church was built in the early 1800s, replacing earlier structures. It became a witness to one of the most turbulent periods in Jamaican history — the 1831–32 Baptist War (also known as the Sam Sharpe Rebellion), the largest slave uprising in the British Caribbean.

The rebellion, led by Samuel Sharpe, began on estates in the hills behind Montego Bay. In the aftermath, Sharpe was executed in the town square. The events hastened the passage of the Abolition Act in 1833. The parish church served as an establishment institution throughout this period.

After Emancipation in 1838, the church continued to serve a changing community. It remains active today as a place of worship and a heritage landmark in Montego Bay.

## Structure/Architecture
The church is built of cut stone in a Georgian style, with thick walls, arched windows, and a square tower. The interior features a traditional nave and chancel with wooden pews and a gallery. Memorial tablets on the walls record the names of planters, soldiers, and officials from the colonial era.

The elevated site provides views over the town and harbour, and the churchyard contains historic graves.

## Worship & Clergy
Regular Anglican services are held, including Sunday Eucharist. The church serves both its local congregation and visitors to Montego Bay who come for its historical significance.

## Interesting Facts
- The church overlooks the town square where Samuel Sharpe, now a National Hero, was executed in 1832.
- The Sam Sharpe Memorial stands in the square below the church.
- St. James Parish Church is a stop on Montego Bay heritage walking tours.
