## Summary
Kingston Parish Church, dedicated to St. Thomas the Apostle, is one of the principal Anglican churches in Jamaica's capital. The present building was rebuilt after the devastating 1907 earthquake.

## History
The first Kingston Parish Church was built soon after the city was founded in 1692, when survivors of the Port Royal earthquake relocated across the harbour. A brick church was erected on the current site in the early eighteenth century and served as the main place of worship for the growing town.

The 1907 Kingston earthquake caused severe damage to the church, and it was subsequently rebuilt in a more earthquake-resistant style. The reconstructed church was consecrated in the early twentieth century and has served the parish continuously since then.

Kingston Parish Church has long been associated with significant events in Jamaican civic and religious life. It sits in the historic downtown area near other colonial-era landmarks.

## Structure/Architecture
The rebuilt church is a substantial brick and concrete structure with a gabled roof and a prominent tower. The interior follows a traditional Anglican layout with nave, chancel, and side aisles. Memorial plaques and stained glass windows commemorate figures from the church's long history.

The churchyard contains historic graves and monuments dating back to the eighteenth century, making it one of the oldest burial grounds in Kingston.

## Worship & Clergy
Services follow the Anglican tradition with regular Sunday worship, Holy Communion, and seasonal observances. The church serves as a centre for community ministry in downtown Kingston.

## Interesting Facts
- The church was founded by refugees from the 1692 Port Royal earthquake.
- The churchyard contains some of the oldest surviving gravestones in Kingston.
- The 1907 earthquake destroyed much of downtown Kingston; the rebuilt church stands as a symbol of the city's resilience.
