## Summary
St. Peter's Church in Port Royal is one of the oldest surviving buildings in this historic harbour town. It was built after the catastrophic 1692 earthquake that destroyed most of Port Royal.

## History
Port Royal was once called "the wickedest city on earth" — a thriving, wealthy port frequented by privateers and merchants in the seventeenth century. On 7 June 1692, a massive earthquake caused much of the town to slide into the sea, killing thousands.

The present St. Peter's Church was erected in 1726 to replace the earlier church lost in the disaster. It has survived subsequent earthquakes, hurricanes, and fires over nearly three centuries, making it one of the most enduring structures in Port Royal.

The church contains artefacts salvaged from the pre-earthquake town and memorials to military and naval figures who served at the nearby Fort Charles. Port Royal's role as a Royal Navy base in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries is reflected in the church's history.

## Structure/Architecture
St. Peter's is a modest brick and stone building with a pitched roof and a small western entrance. The interior is simple, with wooden pews, a chancel, and memorial plaques. A notable feature is the organ loft, and the church houses an old communion plate.

The churchyard contains graves of soldiers, sailors, and residents, some dating to the early eighteenth century.

## Worship & Clergy
Services are held regularly for the small Port Royal community. The church also welcomes visitors drawn to Port Royal's rich and dramatic history.

## Interesting Facts
- The church houses a silver communion set that predates the 1692 earthquake.
- Port Royal sank into the sea during the 1692 earthquake; underwater archaeological remains lie just offshore.
- The church is one of only a few buildings in Port Royal that have survived from the eighteenth century.
